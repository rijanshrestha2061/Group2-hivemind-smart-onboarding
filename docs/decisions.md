# Decisions — Aligned to the 4 Hypotheses

Each decision below explains why a hypothesis was kept, how it was formed, and what alternative was considered.


## Decision: Adopt H1 — Guided, prioritised first steps with visible progress

**What was decided:** Merge the original "guided checklist" and "progress visibility" hypotheses into one combined hypothesis.

**Why:** Both address the same underlying moment in the new-hire journey — knowing what to do and seeing where you stand. P02, P05, and P06 described being given equal-weight access to many systems with no prioritisation; P03 and P06 separately described finishing tasks without knowing what came next. Splitting these into two hypotheses created redundant testing later, since a working prototype naturally needs both together.

**Alternative considered:** Keeping them as two separate hypotheses — rejected because they would be tested with the same interface component in Week 3 anyway.


## Decision: Adopt H2 — Centralised information / single source of truth

**What was decided:** Keep this as its own hypothesis, unmerged.

**Why:** This has the strongest evidence base in the entire research set — all 6 participants (P01, P02, P03, P05, P06, P04) independently described splitting their search across 3+ systems, and 4 of 6 described a specific incident where version confusion cost real time. No other pain point had unanimous support across every interview.

**Alternative considered:** Folding this into H3 (contextual support) since both relate to "finding things" — rejected because centralising information and knowing who to ask are different problems with different solutions (a document index vs. a contact directory).

---

## Decision: Adopt H3 — Trustworthy, contextual support (who to ask + transparent AI)

**What was decided:** Merge the original "contextual help / who to ask" hypothesis with the original "transparent AI support" hypothesis.

**Why:** Both are fundamentally about the same need — getting a reliable answer at the moment of uncertainty, whether that answer comes from a person or an AI. P02 and P06 described not knowing who to ask; separately, all 6 participants required source citations and last-updated dates before trusting an AI answer, and all 6 also agreed AI should hand off to a human for HR, salary, disciplinary, and legal matters. Combining them reflects that "who/what can I trust for help" is one coherent experience, not two.

**Alternative considered:** Dropping the AI-trust half entirely as out of scope — rejected because it was one of only two pain points with unanimous (6/6) support across interviews, too strong a signal to remove.

---

## Decision: Adopt H4 — Role-specific content over generic training

**What was decided:** Keep this as its own hypothesis, unmerged, but note its evidence is narrower than the others.

**Why:** P02, P06, and P04 (3 of 6) independently described mandatory compliance training as necessary but disconnected from actual day-to-day work. This is a real, recurring complaint, but it's less universal than H2 or H3's evidence.

**Alternative considered:** Dropping it to get down to 3 hypotheses — rejected because 3/6 is still a meaningful pattern, and it represents a distinct product idea (content personalisation) not covered by the other three.

---

## Summary

| Hypothesis | Evidence strength | Decision |
|---|---|---|
| H1 — Guided steps + progress | 3/6 participants (P02, P03, P05, P06 combined) | Merged from 2 originals |
| H2 — Centralised info hub | 6/6 participants | Kept standalone — strongest evidence |
| H3 — Trustworthy contextual support | 6/6 participants (AI trust) + 2/6 (who to ask) | Merged from 2 originals |
| H4 — Role-specific content | 3/6 participants | Kept standalone — narrower but distinct |
